"AI" gets stamped on everything these days, so it is fair to be sceptical. But when it comes to **AI route scheduling software**, there is a genuinely useful idea underneath: letting the system decide not just the *order* of stops, but *who* should do them, and getting a better answer than a person could by hand.

## What the "AI" actually does

For a single person with a list of stops, the problem is just ordering — that is [automated route planning](/articles/automated-route-planning). The moment you have a team, the problem gets much harder: every job could go to any person, and each choice changes everyone's route. The number of possible combinations becomes impossible to evaluate manually.

That is where smarter scheduling earns its name. Instead of you manually deciding who takes which job, the system explores thousands of combinations and finds an assignment that keeps total drive time low across the whole team, spreads the workload evenly, and groups nearby jobs onto the same person. Most of the "AI" in route scheduling is advanced optimisation, the same family of techniques used in logistics, applied to your specific jobs and crew. What matters is not the label but whether it gives you a noticeably better plan than you would make yourself.

| | Single route | AI team scheduling |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Decides | Order of one person's stops | Who does what, plus each route |
| Best for | Solo operators | Teams of 2 or more |
| Goal | Least driving for one day | Balanced workload and least driving |
| Re-planning | Reorder one list | Rebalance the whole team |

## Features to look for

Not all "AI scheduling" is equal. When you compare tools, look for whether the system balances workload rather than just minimising total distance, whether it lets you lock or allow job reassignment, and whether it re-optimises instantly when something changes mid-day. The best tools keep you in the driver's seat: they suggest an optimised plan, show you the trade-offs, and let you approve or tweak it rather than silently shuffling your day.

You also need accurate inputs. The system needs real job durations (not guesses) and correct locations. A plan built on rough estimates will look right but run differently in practice.

## Keep a human in the loop

You know things the software does not: which customer prefers which technician, who is training, who avoids certain areas. Good AI scheduling treats its plan as a strong starting point, not a final answer. The goal is to handle the calculation so you can focus on the judgement calls that need a person.
