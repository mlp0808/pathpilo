Every service business owner knows the feeling: the month starts and the pipeline is empty again. You had a good run, the calendar was full, and then it reset. You are back at zero, finding the next round of work.

One-off jobs pay the bills, but they reset you every single month. **Recurring jobs** are the antidote. They are work that repeats automatically, clients who rebook without being asked, and revenue you can plan around. This guide covers how to build a recurring base, which industries lend themselves to it, how to price it, and how to set it up so it genuinely runs without you.

<KeyTakeaways>
- Recurring jobs turn unpredictable one-off income into revenue you can forecast and plan around.
- Retaining an existing customer costs 5 to 7 times less than acquiring a new one.
- Almost any service work can be made recurring — the key is asking at the right moment.
- Book the next visit before you leave the current one. That single habit is the most powerful retention tool.
- A well-set-up recurring schedule effectively runs itself: future visits appear, routes plan, invoices send.
</KeyTakeaways>

## Why recurring revenue changes everything

When you know what is coming in next month, you can plan staff, buy equipment, and take a week off without panic. That stability is worth more than almost any single large job. A business with 20 reliable recurring customers generating predictable monthly revenue is far more resilient than one chasing the same total income from scratch every four weeks.

The psychological shift is significant too. When you are not constantly wondering where the next job is coming from, you make better decisions. You invest in better tools, hire more confidently, and price without desperation.

The financial efficiency also compounds. Research consistently puts the cost of acquiring a new customer at five to seven times the cost of keeping an existing one. Your recurring clients are already bought in, they trust you, they know how you work. You do not need to win them again. Every pound you spend on finding new customers could go further spent keeping the ones you have.

Recurring clients are also better for operations. They are in the same locations week after week, which means your [routes get tighter over time](/articles/cut-drive-time-route-planning). The crew knows the access instructions. There is no new-site uncertainty. Each visit is faster and more efficient than the last.

## What work can be recurring?

More than most business owners think. If a customer needs it once, the only question is when they will need it again — not whether.

<Columns>
  <Column title="Naturally recurring">
  - Cleaning (weekly, fortnightly, monthly)
  - Lawn care and landscaping
  - Pool and pest control
  - Window cleaning
  - Bin cleaning
  </Column>
  <Column title="Can be made recurring">
  - HVAC servicing (seasonal)
  - Plumbing maintenance checks
  - Gutter clearing (twice a year)
  - Equipment inspections
  - Pressure washing (quarterly)
  </Column>
</Columns>

The right conversation is: "How often would you normally want this done?" Most customers have an answer. They just have not been asked in a way that makes recurring the obvious default.

### Industries where recurring is the backbone

**Cleaning**: the highest density of recurring work in any service industry. A domestic cleaning client on a fortnightly schedule is 26 jobs per year. A commercial cleaning contract is often weekly. If you run a cleaning business and fewer than half your clients are on a recurring schedule, you are working harder than you need to.

**Landscaping and lawn care**: seasonal but highly predictable. Spring through autumn typically means weekly or fortnightly visits. Many clients also want a winter tidy-up. A 40-week recurring season with a single client can generate more revenue than 10 one-off jobs across the year.

**Pest control**: most clients want quarterly or bi-annual visits. Once they have used you once and you have delivered, the default is to stay on a plan. Very few clients think to compare alternatives every six months.

**Window cleaning**: the clearest case for recurring. The work resets every four to eight weeks whether the client books or not. A client who books once and gets a good result has the same need in six weeks. The only question is whether they call you or forget and find someone else.

**HVAC and plumbing**: less frequent but very high-value. Annual boiler services, seasonal AC servicing, and pre-winter plumbing checks are natural recurring touchpoints. These clients often also call you first for ad-hoc repairs, which is the most valuable by-product of a recurring relationship.

## How to turn one-off customers into recurring clients

The moment that determines whether a customer recurs is not when they decide to book again. It is the moment the first job finishes.

### Book before you leave

The single most effective habit in building a recurring base: offer the next visit before you leave the current one. The customer is happy. The work is fresh in their mind. They have just experienced the value. This is the moment they are most likely to say yes.

The framing matters. Do not say "would you like to book again?" — that puts the decision entirely on them. Say: "I normally see people like yourself every six weeks. Shall I put you down for the same time in six weeks, and I'll confirm the week before?" Most customers say yes.

If they hesitate, offer a specific date: "I've got a slot on the 14th of next month — I'll book you in and you can always move it if it doesn't work." Specific beats vague. People say yes to a concrete offer more readily than an open-ended one.

<Quote author="Lisa, domestic cleaning" role="12-person team">
When we started booking the next visit before we left, our return rate went from about 40 percent to over 80 percent in three months. The conversation takes 30 seconds. The difference in revenue is enormous.
</Quote>

### Make recurring the default offer, not the upsell

When a new customer enquires, present recurring as the standard option, not an add-on. "We offer a fortnightly clean for £X per visit — most customers find that keeps on top of things without feeling expensive" is a different framing from "We charge £X, and we also offer a regular service."

Position it around convenience for them, not commitment. Customers sign up for things that reduce friction in their life, not things that feel like a contract.

### The loyalty discount

A modest discount for recurring bookings does two things: it makes recurring feel like a reward, and it creates a small psychological lock-in. Five to ten percent off the regular rate for clients who commit to a schedule is typically enough. The discount more than pays for itself through reduced marketing cost and more efficient routes.

<InfoBox type="tip" title="Do not over-discount">
A 5% loyalty discount signals appreciation. A 20% discount signals desperation and trains customers to expect low prices permanently. Keep the discount meaningful but modest, and frame it as a thank-you for reliability, not a price reduction.
</InfoBox>

## How to price recurring work

Recurring work should be priced slightly below your standard one-off rate. The discount reflects the real efficiencies you gain: no marketing cost, predictable scheduling, faster visits as you learn the site.

A useful formula: take your one-off rate and subtract the value of the guaranteed booking. For most service businesses, a 5 to 10 percent reduction is the right range. If you discount more than 10 percent, you are giving away margin that belongs to you.

Price recurring work clearly and simply. A fortnightly clean is £X per visit, paid monthly. An annual boiler service is £Y, booked automatically every November. Avoid complex pricing structures — they create friction and uncertainty, which reduces uptake.

Consider also the full-year value when you are in a pricing conversation. A client worth £120 per visit, once a month, is worth £1,440 per year. That changes how you think about acquisition, retention, and how much flexibility you show on individual visits.

## Setting up recurring jobs: how it works

The operational goal is simple: enter a recurring job once, and have the system generate every future instance automatically.

A well-set-up system lets you:

1. Define the schedule: every week, every two weeks, first Monday of the month, quarterly.
2. Set the duration and assigned crew member.
3. Let future visits appear in the calendar automatically, at the right date and assigned to the right person.
4. Have the route planner include those visits automatically, without any manual addition.
5. Generate an invoice automatically when each visit is marked complete.

This is the closest thing to passive income a service business can have. The work is real and physical, but the administrative overhead approaches zero.

For the system to work well, you need accurate addresses, defined job durations, and a confirmed recurring pattern for each client. Getting this set up for your existing active customers typically takes two to three hours. After that, new recurring clients take about five minutes each to configure.

## Real-world impact: three businesses after six months

### Solo window cleaner: 40% of income now predictable

Daniel ran an ad-hoc window cleaning round. Good months were busy, slow months were stressful. He spent time every week chasing bookings and calling back customers who had not contacted him in a while.

After six months of offering recurring schedules to every customer, 40 percent of his monthly income was pre-booked. He stopped advertising entirely for two months and his income barely changed. The [route efficiency](/articles/automated-route-planning) of his recurring clients, who were clustered by area and visited on rotation, was also significantly better than his previous scattered one-off schedule.

### Cleaning company: scaled without adding admin

Maria's four-person cleaning team was growing, but the admin was growing faster. Booking, rebooking, and chasing irregular clients was taking three to four hours per week.

Switching the team to majority recurring bookings cut her weekly admin to under an hour. Each client's schedule was in the system, invoices generated automatically at the end of each week, and the calendar filled itself for the following week. She was able to take on two additional clients with no meaningful increase in admin time.

### HVAC business: built an annual service base

Tom's HVAC business was almost entirely reactive: customers called when something broke. He started offering annual service plans after every reactive call, and within a year had 80 clients on an annual boiler service. That alone accounted for four full weeks of guaranteed work every autumn, plus a high conversion rate on the subsequent repair calls from those same clients.

## Getting started

If you are starting from nothing, the fastest path is your ten best existing customers. Identify the ones who have used you more than once, who pay without chasing, and who seem satisfied. Call or message them directly:

> "I'm putting together a regular schedule for my best customers — it means I can guarantee your slot and give you a small discount. Would you like me to set you up on a regular [monthly/quarterly] plan?"

Most will say yes. The ones who do become the core of your recurring base. As you add new customers going forward, recurring should be the first offer, not the afterthought.

Once recurring clients start filling the calendar, [invoicing them consistently and automatically](/articles/get-paid-faster-service-invoices) becomes the next priority. Manual invoicing for 20 recurring clients is still manageable. For 40 or 60, it needs to be automatic.

## Frequently asked questions

### How do I handle recurring jobs when a client wants to skip a visit?

Most clients who skip occasionally are not cancelling the recurring relationship — they are deferring one visit. A good system lets you skip a single instance without affecting the pattern. The next visit still generates automatically on the scheduled date. Handle skips gracefully and without friction; making it easy to skip occasionally makes clients more comfortable committing to a recurring schedule in the first place.

### Should I require a contract for recurring clients?

For high-frequency, high-value clients (commercial contracts, regular maintenance plans), a short service agreement is reasonable and protects both sides. For domestic recurring clients, most service businesses operate without formal contracts. A clear email confirmation of the schedule and rate is sufficient in most cases and removes the friction of a formal sign-up process.

### How much of a discount should I offer for recurring bookings?

The right range for most service businesses is 5 to 10 percent. Below 5 percent, the incentive is not meaningful. Above 10 percent, you are eroding margin significantly for work you were going to get anyway. Frame the discount as a loyalty reward, not a price reduction, and it retains more of its perceived value.

### What if a recurring client stops responding?

Check their history first. If there is no complaint or issue logged, the most common reasons are that they forgot about the next visit, life changed temporarily, or they simply moved on without saying anything. A brief personal message — not a generic reminder — re-engages a meaningful percentage. If they do not respond after two attempts, remove them from the recurring schedule to keep your calendar accurate.

### Can I run recurring and one-off jobs in the same system?

Yes, and you should. One-off jobs fill gaps in the recurring schedule, handle urgent calls, and bring in new clients who may convert to recurring later. The goal is not to eliminate one-off work — it is to ensure your calendar has a predictable recurring floor underneath the variable one-off income on top.

### How do recurring jobs affect route planning?

Positively. Recurring clients in the same area, visited on a consistent schedule, produce increasingly tight routes over time. The system knows exactly where they are, how long each visit takes, and when to plan them. Over months, a well-structured recurring schedule develops into a highly efficient route that fits more work into the same time than the equivalent one-off schedule ever could.
