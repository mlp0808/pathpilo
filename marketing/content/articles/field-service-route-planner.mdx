Most field service route planners are built for logistics companies running 50 vehicles, not for a 3-van plumbing team or a solo cleaning contractor. The enterprise tools are overbuilt and overpriced; the basic consumer apps don't understand service work. Small field service teams fall in between, and finding the right tool requires knowing what to look for.

This is a practical buyer's guide for service businesses with 1 to 15 field workers. It covers what to look for, what to skip, a features comparison table, and how to evaluate during a free trial without getting misled by demos.

<KeyTakeaways>
- Field service route planners are distinct from logistics routing tools — they need to understand jobs, clients, and recurring schedules, not just addresses.
- The five features that matter most for small teams: map-based assignment, AI stop ordering, real-time mobile sync, recurring job support, and connected invoicing.
- Avoid tools that only handle routing without integrating job management — you'll spend too much time transferring data between systems.
- Free tiers are available and genuinely useful for testing, but check which features are locked before evaluating.
- For most small field service businesses, the right tool pays for itself in two weeks of reduced drive time and planning overhead.
</KeyTakeaways>

## What makes a route planner "field service" vs generic routing?

Generic routing tools (like Google Maps multi-stop or standalone route optimisers) handle the question of "what order should I visit these addresses?" They don't understand that each stop is a job with a duration, a client record, a billing relationship, and potentially a recurring schedule.

A **field service route planner** connects routing to the operational context around each stop:

- Each pin on the map is a job, not just an address — it has a duration, a service type, notes, and a client
- Jobs come from a schedule, not from manual address entry each morning
- Completed jobs feed through to invoicing, not just route history
- Recurring jobs re-appear automatically in the calendar and route

This distinction is what separates a field service route planner from a general-purpose tool. If your business does repeat work, has a client database, or invoices after jobs, you need the field service version.

## The features that matter for small teams

### The non-negotiables

<InfoBox type="info" title="Must-have features checklist">
✅ **Map view with all jobs visible** — see every stop on a map, not just a list

✅ **AI stop ordering** — the system calculates the optimal sequence, not you

✅ **Job duration support** — the planner accounts for how long each stop takes

✅ **Mobile app for field workers** — technicians get their route on their phone

✅ **Real-time status sync** — the office can see what's happening in the field

✅ **Recurring job support** — repeat work re-appears automatically, no manual re-entry
</InfoBox>

**Map view with all jobs visible** is the starting point. You should be able to see every scheduled job on a map before planning begins. Without this, you're planning blind — making assignment decisions without understanding the geography.

**AI stop ordering** is what separates a route planner from a scheduling calendar. It should calculate the optimal visit sequence for a full day in under a second, using real drive times (not distances) and accounting for job durations.

**Job duration support** is often missing in basic tools and it matters more than it looks. Without knowing each job takes 45 minutes, the system cannot tell you whether a 10-stop day is actually achievable in an 8-hour window.

### The features that matter more as you grow

**Multi-technician view** — can you see all your technicians' routes on the same map simultaneously? For a 2-person team this is useful but not critical. For a 5-person team it's essential for balancing workload geographically.

**Time window constraints** — some customers can only be visited during specific windows. Can you set this on a job-by-job basis and have the route planner respect it during optimisation?

**Same-day re-planning** — when a job cancels or an urgent job is added mid-day, does the system reoptimise the remaining route automatically? Or do you have to manually reshuffle?

**Automatic customer notifications** — when a technician starts the next job, can the system send an automated ETA to the customer? This is a small feature that eliminates a large number of "when are you arriving?" calls.

### The features you can safely skip at small scale

- **Fleet tracking GPS overlays** — useful at large scale, overkill for small teams where you're already in contact with technicians
- **Advanced territory management** — becomes relevant at 10+ technicians, unnecessary below that
- **Custom API integrations** — only relevant if you're building a tech stack, not if you just need one tool that works

## How to evaluate during a free trial

### Week 1: Test the core workflow
Create 5–10 jobs from your real client list. Assign them across your team members. Run the AI route optimiser. Check:
- Does the suggested route match your understanding of local geography?
- Does it account for how long each job actually takes?
- How long does it take the technician to see the updated route on their phone?

### Week 2: Test the edge cases
Test what happens when:
- A job cancels and you need to reoptimise the remaining day
- An urgent job comes in and you need to add it mid-afternoon
- A customer requests a specific time window

These scenarios reveal the gaps faster than any demo.

### Before committing: test the support
Send a support question during the trial. The quality of the response — speed, specificity, whether they actually understood what you asked — tells you more about what using the tool long-term will be like than any feature list.

<InfoBox type="tip" title="The 10-minute rule">
If it takes more than 10 minutes to plan the next day's routes after your first week of use, the tool is either the wrong fit or needs more configuration. A good field service route planner should compress daily planning to under 10 minutes for most small teams by week two.
</InfoBox>

## Field service installation routing: a specific consideration

If your work involves installations — equipment fitting, initial setups, commissioning visits — you have additional requirements beyond standard service routing:

- **Variable job durations**: installations often take significantly longer than standard service calls. The planner needs accurate durations to avoid scheduling unrealistic days.
- **Site preparation dependencies**: you may need a survey before the installation. Some systems support job dependencies (job B can't be scheduled until job A is complete).
- **Parts and equipment logistics**: if technicians carry specific equipment to installation jobs, the routing needs to account for who has the right equipment in their van.

Not all field service route planners handle these requirements. If installations are a significant part of your work, confirm these capabilities before committing.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the best field service route planner for small teams?

There's no single answer because "best" depends on your specific context — team size, job types, whether you do recurring work. The key evaluation criteria for small teams are: does it connect routing to job management, does it have a reliable mobile app, does it handle recurring jobs, and does it cost under £50/month for a 2–3 person team?

### Is there a free field service route planner?

Yes. Most platforms have a free plan that covers the basic workflow. The question is whether the free plan includes AI optimisation and the mobile app — without those, you can't fully test whether the tool is right for you. PathPilo offers a free plan that includes core routing and scheduling features.

### Can a route planner handle both field service jobs and product deliveries?

Most field service platforms are optimised for service work (variable durations, client relationships, recurring visits). If you're mixing service calls with physical deliveries (parcels, stock restocking), you may need a logistics tool that handles both. If the "deliveries" are delivery-of-service (delivering a cleaning, a maintenance visit), a standard field service platform is the right choice.

### How long does it take to set up a field service route planner?

For a small team (1–5 technicians), initial setup — importing your client list, adding services, and running your first route — takes 1–3 hours. Most platforms have guided onboarding. Running time after that is under 10 minutes per day for planning.
