## The problem with treating routing and job management as separate tools

Many service businesses start with two separate tools: a job management app (to track what needs doing) and a mapping or navigation app (to figure out how to get there efficiently). The gap between them is where time gets lost.

The morning looks like this: open job management, check what's on today, export or manually note the addresses, switch to the route planner, enter each address, get the order, switch back to the job management app, manually update each job with the sequence, message every technician their list. That is 20–40 minutes of overhead for something that should take 2 minutes.

**Job routing software** eliminates this gap. Because the job management layer and the routing layer share the same database, a job's address is a map coordinate from the moment it's created. You never re-enter it.

## How it works: from job creation to technician on the road

### Step 1: The job is created and geotagged

When you create a new job — either manually, from a recurring schedule, or via an online booking — the software records the client's address and immediately geocodes it: converts the text address into a latitude and longitude coordinate. This happens in the background in under a second.

That coordinate is what the routing algorithm uses. Every job on your system has a map location the moment it exists.

<InfoBox type="info" title="Why geocoding at creation time matters">
Some tools only geocode when you open the route planner. This creates a delay at planning time and means that if an address is slightly wrong (a transposed postcode, a missing flat number), you don't find out until you're trying to plan the route — not when you could still fix it easily. Good job routing software geocodes immediately and flags address errors at creation.
</InfoBox>

### Step 2: You open the map and see everything at once

The routing view shows a live map with every scheduled job plotted as a pin, and every available technician as a marker (their last known location or their home address if they haven't started). This is **job management route mapping** — the plan is visual from the start, not a list that gets mapped later.

You can immediately see:
- Which jobs are geographically close to each other (clustering opportunities)
- Which technicians are physically near which jobs (assignment opportunities)
- Which technician's day is already packed vs still has capacity

This context — which is completely invisible in a list view — is what makes location-based assignment fast and accurate.

### Step 3: Assign jobs to the nearest available technician

In the map view, you select an unassigned job and see which technicians are nearby. One click assigns it. The technician's route immediately updates to include the new job and recalculates the optimal stop order.

This is the core of **field service automation software that assigns jobs by location**: the system shows you the geography, you make the assignment, the routing is instant. The manual step of "figure out who's closest" is replaced by the map doing it visually. The manual step of "update the route" is replaced by the system doing it automatically.

### Step 4: AI optimises the full route

After assignment, either manually or on demand, the AI route optimiser runs on the technician's full day. It considers every assigned stop, their real-road drive times, each job's duration, and any time windows you've specified. The output is the optimal stop order for the day.

You review it on the map, apply any local knowledge adjustments (locked time windows, a customer who prefers afternoon visits), and confirm. That confirmed route appears on the technician's mobile app instantly.

### Step 5: The field team executes, the office sees everything live

The technician works from the mobile app: start job, complete job, add notes, move on. Every status change syncs back to the office in real time. You can see who is on-site, who is driving to the next stop, and when each job was completed — without making a single phone call.

When a job is marked complete, an invoice can be generated automatically, the client's record is updated, and if it was a recurring job, the next visit is already in the calendar.

## What "job management route mapping" means in practice

The phrase **job management route mapping** describes a feature set that is more than just "see jobs on a map." It means:

- **Every job has a map coordinate** from creation, not just a text address.
- **The map is the primary planning interface** — you plan in the map, not in a list.
- **Assignment and routing are the same action** — assigning a job updates the route automatically.
- **The map reflects reality in real time** — job statuses update as the field team works.

This is distinct from a basic "show my jobs on a map" feature, which is just a visual layer on top of a list. True job management route mapping means the map is the workflow, not a view mode.

## Handling the hardest real-world scenario: same-day additions

The clearest test of job routing software is not the calm morning plan — it's what happens when an urgent job is added at 11am.

Without good software: you call your nearest available technician to check their location, work out mentally whether the new job fits, call again to update them, and manually adjust their afternoon sequence.

With job routing software: you create the job (it's immediately on the map), see which technician is closest and has capacity, assign with one click, and the system recalculates their remaining afternoon route around the new stop. The technician gets a notification on their phone. The whole process takes 90 seconds.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is job routing software?

Job routing software is a field service management tool that combines job management (creating, tracking, and completing work orders) with location-based assignment and route optimisation. When you create a job, it's immediately on the map. When you assign it, the technician's route updates automatically.

### How is this different from a basic route planner?

A basic route planner optimises a sequence of addresses you enter manually. Job routing software connects that optimisation to a live job management system — jobs flow directly from creation into the routing layer, and completed jobs flow back to invoicing and scheduling. There is no manual transfer between tools.

### Can it automatically assign jobs without any human input?

Some platforms offer fully automated dispatch — the system decides which technician to assign based on proximity, skills, and capacity without human input. Most tools, including PathPilo, show you the geography and let you assign with one click, keeping you in control of the decision while removing all the manual logistics.

### Does it work for a business with just one or two technicians?

Yes. The efficiency gain from seeing your jobs on a map and clicking to assign is visible from team sizes of one. You can't assign to "the nearest technician" if there's only one, but the route optimisation and real-time sync are equally valuable for solo operators and large teams.
