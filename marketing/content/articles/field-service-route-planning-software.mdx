If your team drives to customers, the route is the hidden cost centre of your business. Every extra kilometre driven is fuel burned and time lost. Every morning spent hand-planning the day is capacity that could be on the road. The right **field service route planning software** turns that cost into capacity, fitting more jobs into the same day without hiring more people.

This guide helps you choose one without drowning in feature checklists or overpaying for tools built to manage national delivery fleets, not a three-van cleaning company.

## What route planning software is actually for

At its simplest, it answers a daily question: what is the best order to visit today's jobs, and who should take each one?

Done well, it removes that question entirely. The system looks at every address on the day's list, every job's duration, your crew's starting points, and real-road traffic. It outputs an ordered route in under a second. You review it, adjust anything your local knowledge requires, and go.

Done badly, or not at all, you leak time and fuel every single day. Studies across logistics and field service consistently find that manually planned routes use 20 to 30 percent more drive time than optimised ones. For a team doing 8 stops a day in a 20-mile radius, that is typically 40 to 60 minutes of wasted driving per person.

## The features that actually matter

Vendors list dozens of features. Here is what separates a tool you will use every day from one you will abandon after three weeks.

The most important item on this list is the mobile app, not the algorithm. A theoretically superior optimiser running on a desktop app that your crew refuses to open is worse than a slightly simpler tool they check every morning from the van. The adoption test (have a real crew member use the app for an hour, then ask if they would use it every day) is more revealing than any feature comparison.

## Standalone tools versus integrated platforms

This is the decision that most buyers make without realising they are making it.

A **standalone route optimiser** does one thing well: it takes a list of addresses and returns an ordered route. Some are excellent. The problem is that your addresses need to come from somewhere (usually a spreadsheet), and when the route is done, your completed jobs need to go somewhere (usually another app, or nothing). Every handoff is a place where information gets lost and time gets spent.

An **integrated field service platform** handles scheduling, client records, routing, and invoicing in a single system. The route planner can see job durations and time windows from the job record. Completed jobs automatically populate invoices. Client notes (access codes, dog at the property, preferred arrival window) are visible to the crew on the same screen as the route.

## Five questions to ask before you buy

The best software is the one your team actually uses. Before committing, run the adoption test: have a real crew member try the app for an hour using real jobs. If they resist it, no feature list will save the implementation.

Beyond that, ask vendors these five questions directly. They will not volunteer the answers unprompted.

### 1. Does it optimise for time or distance?

These are not the same. A shorter route on a map can take longer if it goes through a city centre at rush hour. Good route planning uses real-road travel times with time-of-day traffic weighting. If the answer is "we optimise by distance", the routes will look tidy on paper and run late in practice.

### 2. Can it re-plan after a cancellation or a same-day addition?

A job drops out at 10am. A new urgent call comes in at 11am. The system should recalculate the affected route in under a second. If re-planning requires you to rebuild the whole day from scratch, you will stop using it the moment things get busy.

### 3. Does routing connect to your jobs and invoicing, or is it a separate silo?

Ask specifically: "If I mark a job as complete on the route, does that trigger an invoice?" and "Does the route planner see job durations and time windows from the job record?" If the answer to either is no, you are looking at a standalone optimiser dressed up as a platform.

### 4. How does pricing scale as you add people?

Some tools charge per user, which sounds reasonable until you add a third van and your cost triples. Others charge per route, per stop, or on a flat monthly rate. Understand the scaling model before you grow into a pricing surprise.

### 5. Is there a free trial with real data?

A demo is a polished presentation. A trial with your own jobs, your own customers, and your actual crew is how you know if it works for you. Any vendor unwilling to let you run a real trial for two weeks has reason to believe the product will not hold up.

## How to evaluate during a free trial

Most buyers use a free trial to look around the interface. That is not enough. Run this test instead:

1. **Import one week of real jobs.** Not example data — your actual customers and addresses from last week.
2. **Run the route for one day alongside your normal manual planning.** Do not use the optimised route; just compare it to what you would have done.
3. **Note the difference in total drive time.** Most teams find the optimised version is 25 to 40 percent shorter. If it is not, the system does not have enough information (check that job durations and start points are set correctly).
4. **Have one crew member use the mobile app for two full days.** Ask them: "Would you use this every morning?" Their answer is your decision.

<InfoBox type="tip" title="The one-week data import is the real test">
If you cannot get your existing customer data into the tool within 30 minutes, the setup friction is too high. Any credible platform makes this straightforward — CSV import or a quick-start flow that takes a new customer 2 minutes to enter.
</InfoBox>

## Right-sizing for your business

Many owners overbuy, choosing an enterprise dispatch suite built for regional logistics companies and using 10 percent of it. The other 90 percent sits unused and the interface feels designed for someone else's problems. Others underbuy, bolting a standalone mapping app onto a spreadsheet and still spending an hour manually planning routes every morning.

The sweet spot for most service businesses is a connected [field service management platform](/articles/field-service-management-software-guide) where routing is one piece alongside scheduling, client records, and invoicing — not a separate tool you switch between.

Shortlist two or three tools. Run the trial with real work and real data. Check that routing genuinely connects to the rest of your operations. The winner is rarely the one with the longest feature list. It is the one your team opens every morning without being asked.

## Frequently asked questions

### How much does field service route planning software cost?

Entry-level tools start at around £20 to £40 per month for a single user. Integrated field service platforms that include routing, scheduling, and invoicing typically range from £40 to £120 per month for small teams. Enterprise dispatch suites aimed at large fleets can cost several hundred pounds per month per user. Most small service businesses do not need the enterprise tier.

### Do I need route planning software if I only have one person?

Yes. Even a solo operator benefits significantly. Automated routing removes the daily planning task (typically 15 to 30 minutes), and the optimised route order consistently outperforms manual planning by 15 to 30 percent. The ROI is usually positive within the first month.

### What is the difference between route planning and route optimisation?

Route planning means deciding which jobs to do in which order. Route optimisation means finding the *best* possible order algorithmically. All route planning software includes some form of optimisation, but the quality varies: basic tools sort by distance, better ones optimise for travel time including traffic, and the best also account for job durations, time windows, and crew start and end points.

### Can it handle multiple crew members at once?

Yes — most tools can optimise routes for multiple people simultaneously. The more advanced platforms use [AI route scheduling](/articles/ai-route-scheduling-software) to decide not just *how* to order each person's day, but *who* should take which job in the first place, based on location, skill, and workload balance.

### What happens when a job is cancelled last minute?

Good systems allow instant re-planning of the affected route without rebuilding the whole day. You remove the cancelled job, and the system recalculates the remaining stops in a few seconds. This is one of the most important real-world features to test during a trial.

### Does it work offline?

Mobile apps vary. Some cache the day's route locally so crew can see it without a signal; others require connectivity to load the route. If your crew works in areas with poor coverage (industrial sites, rural properties), check the offline behaviour explicitly before committing.

### How long does setup take?

Most small teams are running optimised routes within two to three hours. The main investment is entering existing customer data. If you have 20 to 40 regular customers, expect one to two hours of data entry to get the full benefit. After that, new customers take two minutes each.
