Most service businesses lose more time to **driving** than to the actual work. A plumber, cleaner, or landscaper with eight stops a day can easily spend two to three hours behind the wheel, and a big chunk of that is avoidable.

The good news: route planning is one of the few areas where a small change pays off every single day. Order your stops well and you reclaim hours each week without working any harder.

## Why drive time quietly kills your margins

Every minute a van is moving is a minute you cannot bill. Worse, fuel, vehicle wear, and the mental fatigue of bad traffic all stack up. Yet most teams still plan the day in the order jobs were booked, which is almost never the order that makes geographic sense.

The fix is straightforward: think of your day as a loop that starts and ends at your base. Visit the furthest cluster of jobs first, then work your way back. You will rarely cross your own path. Groups of stops that are geographically close should go back to back, regardless of when the customer booked.

## Plan by geography, not by booking order

For three or four stops, you can do this by hand in a few minutes. Past five or six stops, the combinations multiply and the mental effort of finding the best order starts to eat into your morning. For multiple crew members covering overlapping areas, it becomes nearly impossible to get right manually.

Automatic route optimisation solves that problem: the system looks at every stop, the time each job takes, and current traffic, then produces the best order in under a second. With [PathPilo's route planning](/features/routeplanning) you can also drag a job to a different crew member and watch both routes rebalance immediately.

## A simple weekly routine

| Day part | What to do | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Night before | Lock tomorrow's stops and auto-plan the route | Crew can leave straight from home |
| Morning | Re-optimise if anything changed overnight | One cancellation can reshuffle the whole loop |
| During the day | Track live progress and ETAs | Spot a slipping schedule before the customer calls |
| End of week | Review actual vs. planned drive time | Find the routes worth tightening further |
